Quark Obscurity

Visual Design | Marketing Strategy | Web UI/UX

Quark Security, Inc. (Quark) is a defense contractor focusing on mobile security applications and SELinux engineering. They were acquired in 2019 by Sealing Technologies, Inc. (SealingTech) in December 2019, and are currently in the process of merging operations. In addition to being a designer on the SealingTech end, I was able to provide the Quark team with design solutions to help ease the assimilation over from their previous Quark identity and welcome them into SealingTech's brand.

Quark Obscurity Redesign

Quark had been previously working on a consumer-level MDM application for Android before the merge with SealingTech. In November of 2020, the Quark team picked up on Obscurity's development and predicted a launch date of early 2021. As it was difficult to run a campaign when the app carried a different visual identity, I was put in charge of doing a rebrand for Obscurity.

The biggest challenge was that despite changing the logo and implementing their content into SealingTech's collateral style, the app itself still looked different and there was no design team or prototyping process on the development end. I overlapped my web design knowledge into mobile UX, studied Android's material design, and ensured the Obscurity team had sufficient design systems to reference.

Original Homepage. Note the lack of branding, content, and confusing navigation.

Scope

  • Consumer + Competitor Research
  • Logo Redesign
  • Establish Design System
  • Visual Prototyping
  • Alpha User Testing
  • Landing Page + Marketing Collateral
  • Campaign Launch

    • Research

    Generally, most current-market MDMs are enterprise-level and cater to organizations that offer employee devices. A unique factor I found was that with the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic, MDMs have a new opportunity to be utilized at a consumer level for parents who might need amped-up security on their children's devices for protection and monitoring usage with entertainment and remote learning. Quark Obscurity's level of detail in custom functions and monitoring settings were beyond the easy usability of new competitors, but because of its intricacy, it provided features useful for parents with a tech-savvy background who need those extra layers in securing family devices.

    Process

    Although I was part of the initial planning and design stages, I was reallocated to a dedicated high-priority project at the time and mockups were reassigned to a contract designer. My role was reassigned to me in a time crunch 2 months til launch due to web development and brand consistency conflicts with prior mockups.

    I successfully created a design system, high fidelity mockups, and rehashed/created new graphic assets to fit our brand. There were a few changes made with guidance from the stakeholders that are live on our current website, and a few extra pages were improvised by the developers using my mockups as reference.

    Navigation and content planning done on LucidChart.

    Wireframes and rough visual ideas.

    Final Thoughts

    I am glad the relaunch was successful despite the timecrunch! Because of our revamped web presence, we were able to increase engagement and bring in conversions. Overall, we made a huge improvement in comparison to the original website and it was well-received by the company and our customers. Personally, this was a journey where I realized that I wanted to grow as a UX designer and experienced the differences and overlap between UX Design and Marketing first-hand.

    Revisiting the project a few months after, there were a few aspects I would consider changing with the live website to help with visual improvements, user accessibility, and mobile responsiveness, specifically for the home and product pages.

    Original homepage (left), conceptual revision (right).

    I moved the Service section to the top to sum up the company and bridge some of the information over to a product highlight for our Fly Away Kits, which would be considered a Featured Product section rather than repeating the Products page. While the approved version displayed a lot of red, I toned the usage down for easier indication of CTAs and resource links.

    Original product page revision (left), conceptual revision (right).

    I utilized the hero image container from the other pages for layout consistency. Since I wanted to divide up the space, I added an off-white background and fullscreen icon for clearer indication of a 360-degree preview module. The product description/CTA row was revised for cleaner spacing and mobile responsiveness, as well as information hierarchy. I condensed the "Industry" section for less scrolling.
