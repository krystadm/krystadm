Quark Obscurity

After the Quark Security merger with SealingTech, the product team pushed for an early 2021 launch of Quark's consumer-level MDM security application for Android™, Quark Obscurity. Because of the merger, the product engineering team conducted internal tests to address certain usability issues and also aimed for a UI redesign to be cohesive with SealingTech's brand identity.

View App Page → View Landing Page →

.01 Overview

How might we receive the insights to improve the design, usability, and launch a product in an evolving market?

Duration

November 2020 – January 2021

Role

Visual Design, Interaction Design, Marketing Design

Tools

Adobe XD, Adobe Illustrator, Pardot, Wordpress

Problem

Putting a complex mobile device management for mobile security app back on the front-burner is a difficult feat in a busy and rapidly evolving market when design frameworks and usability are changing at the same rate. With the added difficulties of a merger brings the need to evolve a visual identity as well. With limited time and resources.

Solution

Utilize past and qualitative insights to rapid reiterate changes, implement the parent acquisitions visual identity, and produce a campaign to encourage open testing and continuous improve of the application.

.02 Understand

Synthesizing Old and New Research

To get a general idea of the MDM consumer market and user-base, I researched relative consumer MDM competitors, their use cases, and general design trends for the MDM/Security app market. Based on existing data from before Quark's merger, I learned that Quark Obscurity's initial testing was conducted with a group from UMD's Computer Science department. Comparing this data with the complexity of the app itself showed that this product aimed towards a user that had a technical background and needed purely customizable security settings for their portable devices.

Competitive Audit

MDM competitors had less customizability on a consumer level, and those that did offer customizability operated through an enterprise pricing model not targeted towards individuals.

.03 Define

Defining the Redesign

Because the app was already developed and had a rushed launch date, a few solutions I had come up with regarding the main UI were able to be implemented, but the public-facing app generally stayed the same because of development capabilities. Not much had changed in the public-facing app besides the colors and typography and component states referenced by the design system which was based on Material Design and specified styles for certain assets, typography, and interaction flows.

Design Guidelines

I created this guide for our engineering team so they could utilize some of the styling rules for future improvements or build upon it.

.04 Ideate

Ideating the Solution

A prototype for the application did not exist, so I decided to create one in Adobe XD to avoid the lengthy process of relaying changes and experimenting with the code directly in the future. As a challenge to myself, I decided that I would just redesign the app and implement the proposed changes in the XD prototype that were approved by stakeholders, but were not present in the prior stage of development.

High-Fidelity Mockups

I built a high-fidelity rendition to demo the styling guidelines for the product, especially on the component states and modules, and demonstrate the animations present in navigating through the app.

.05 Support

Marketing the Product

Unfortunately, there wasn't much I could do further for the product team at the time because of their testing state. So I shifted over to helping market the product to get more insights from alpha and beta testing.

So what does this have to do with UX?

It was up to me to redesign the interactive processes that went into building interest, such as the custom forms, surveys, emails, and landing pages. I also utilized the technical documentation that the product team was using to copywrite friendly, informative content that would teach users a bit more about the app.

Popup or Embedded Form?

The parent SealingTech website had been using pop-up forms since the relaunch, so we ultimately decided to A/B Test the effects on having an "embedded form" version to see how it affected our conversion rate.

I also introduced new interactive elements which would later be applied to every form on the website. Each field would be have a border when active and display an icon correlating to the field.

The popup form was later reused as a survey template to be linked to through email.

A Final Landing Page to Rule Them All

I came up with 3 landing page concepts with embedded forms (pictured: newest to oldest) to promote Obscurity's features and get users to sign up. Eventually it was decided that Obscurity's page would be under SealingTech's domain so the style evolved to compliment its parent brand over time.

.06 Deliver

Delivering the Final Result

After multiple interations throughout the ideation phase, stakeholders decided on the final prototype for the sign up forms, website, and key guidelines that the development team on the product-end would use in the launch of Obscurity. I worked with our web development and marketing agency teams to ensure the mockups would translate as accurately as possible. While there were limitations on the product-end from fully implementing my designs, I was glad to see that some of the guidelines made it in the final product.

Final Mockups

Final delivery for my marketing deliverables and redesigned prototype, which demo a reimagined onboarding, restyled guidelines, and updated components.

.07 Reflect

Reflecting the Obscure

This was my first exposure to product design and was the bridge into me learning more about interaction and component design. As a full-stack designer for SealingTech, there weren't many opportunities for me to delve more into interaction design besides the website, so when my role evolved into helping the engineering and products team directly, I was excited to learn about a form of design that would later be my new concentration. It definitely wasn't easy and there was a lot I wish I could have assisted with.

Lessons Learned

  • Existing frameworks exist and are there to help!
  • MDMs are difficult to make "easy"
  • Research, testing, and insights are important

What I Would Do Differently

  • Advocating for more insights to be applied to final product
  • Develop a system that is easier for handoff
  • Push for more heuristics and consulting
